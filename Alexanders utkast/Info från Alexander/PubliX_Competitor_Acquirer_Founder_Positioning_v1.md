# Competitor acquirers — how they position to founders, and their website CTAs

Scan of how seven rival software acquirers pitch themselves to selling founders, and the call to action each uses on its seller-facing page. Built to inform PubliX's own founder-facing messaging and outreach. All competitor positioning quoted below is taken from the live seller-facing pages, fetched 2026-06-12 (Verified). The PubliX positioning lines are from the board materials cited above.

## The seven, side by side

| Acquirer | Type / fit to PubliX | Positioning to founders (their words) | Call to action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Marathon Software** (SE) | Closest comparable. Permanent-capital holder, SEK 5-50M revenue niche Nordic software. | "The long-term home for niche software companies." Investing "without an end date"; "no time-limited funds which would force us to sell"; operational engagement. Prefers "a joint journey where key individuals are with us as minority owners." | "Want to discuss a company? Let's talk" — direct email to a named team. Soft, owner-to-founder. |
| **Pamir Partners** (SE) | Closest comparable. Independent long-term owner of Nordic B2B SaaS, majority stakes, can take minority. | "We would like to buy your software company, in whole or in part... sell to someone you trust; someone who can perpetuate your legacy." Frames the sale as the founder's "next step" — step back, or stay and scale. "We seek value, not bargains." | "We buy companies" landing pitch; contact-led enquiry. No hard funnel. |
| **Lyvia Group** (SE) | Adjacent. Nordic/European tech-and-software group, acquisitive since 2020. | "No one is better qualified to run a business than the entrepreneur that created it." Long-term owner, "not a partnership with the sole ambition of selling." | Contact form with an "I'm a: Entrepreneur" selector — the only one with founder-segmented intake. Dedicated "For entrepreneurs" page. |
| **Vitec Software Group** (SE) | Listed compounder, broad multi-vertical. Above PubliX's size band. | "We are entrepreneurs with a long-term perspective... entrepreneurs who have a dialogue with other entrepreneurs. We are not an investment company." Company keeps developing and stays part of the Group. | "The easiest way to get in touch is to call or email us directly." One named M&A contact with phone and email. No form. |
| **Visma** (NO) | Largest Nordic business-software owner. Different scale, overlapping message. | "We don't change who they are. We amplify all they can be." Thinks "in decades"; "you keep leading, we provide the rest"; peer founder network — "call anyone we've acquired in the last 15 years." Screens for "cloud-native and AI-native." | "Ready for your next chapter? Connect with our M&A team." Named Director of M&A with email. |
| **Constellation Software** (CA) | Global permanent owner of VMS; the systematic-acquirer benchmark. | Buys "Good" and rarely "Exceptional" VMS firms; founders selling for "health, estate planning or a burgeoning interest elsewhere" who want the business to keep its history and values. "We will not take over the day-to-day management." | Publishes criteria plus philosophy: "strictest confidence," reply "within one business day," "no unfriendly takeovers." Enquiry by email. |
| **Chapters Group** (DE) | European permanent-capital consolidator; succession play. | "Permanent home" for mission-critical software; explicit succession angle on the ageing-founder problem; founders "value their legacy as much as the purchase price." | Permanent-capital / hold-forever framing; low-intensity, contact-led. |

## What the scan shows

**The script is shared, almost word for word.** Permanence, founder trust, legacy continuation, and operating autonomy run through all seven. Vitec, Constellation, Lyvia, Marathon and Pamir each state plainly that the founder keeps running the company. This undercuts the internal shorthand that splits the field into "buy and leave alone" (Vitec) versus "absorb into platform" (Visma): Visma's own page now reads like a permanent owner ("we don't change who they are," "you keep leading"). The honest contrast with Visma is governance depth and earnout/reinvestment economics, not autonomy messaging.

**The calls to action are deliberately low-friction.** Every one routes to a named human, promises or implies confidentiality, and avoids valuation bait. None runs a hard lead-gen funnel. Constellation's "strictest confidence, reply within one business day" and Vitec's "just call or email us directly" set the register: trust-led, not transactional. Lyvia's "I'm an Entrepreneur" intake selector is the single mechanic worth copying.

**AI is open space to founders — with one mover.** Only Visma foregrounds "AI-native" (in selection criteria and an Innovation-and-AI commitment). The other six say nothing to founders about AI. Marathon and Pamir, the two competitors PubliX actually meets in deals, are silent on it.

**The two closest comparables are Marathon and Pamir, not the listed names.** Both target the same profile PubliX hunts: profitable, founder-owned Nordic niche software, broadly SEK 5-50M revenue (Verified for Marathon; Pamir states Nordic B2B SaaS without a public band). When a Swedish founder at SEK 10-30M weighs offers, these two are the likely names in the room, not Constellation or Vitec. Both sharpen the generic script: Marathon with concrete minority co-ownership for key people, Pamir with "in whole or in part" plus "we seek value, not bargains" to disarm both the stay-on founder and the lowball worry.

## Implications for PubliX

The offensive pitch — PubliX as a growth engine for founders who want to compete and win in the AI age, with central AI capability and commercial enablement — differentiates cleanly against six of the seven today. It does not differentiate against Visma, which is closing the AI angle in its own founder messaging. Treat the AI-native pitch as a roughly 12-month edge against Visma specifically, and a durable edge against Marathon, Pamir, Vitec, Constellation and Chapters only for as long as the proof points stay ahead.

That edge is conditional, not owned. It rests on proof points being demonstrable in founder conversations: Aspicore live with LLM grant grading, Koivu apps in testing, a delivered enablement track record. The board note targets Q3 2026 for this. If those slip, the pitch reduces to claims, set against two credible Nordic permanent-home competitors (Marathon, Pamir) saying much the same thing on autonomy, legacy, and long-term ownership.

Two messaging corrections follow. First, drop "unlike Visma, we preserve autonomy" from seller-facing material — the contrast no longer holds on the page and a sharp founder will know it. Compete with Visma on governance depth, AI capability, and deal economics instead. Second, match the category's CTA register. A hard SaaS-style funnel would read as off-key here; route founders to a named person, lead with confidentiality, and consider Lyvia's founder-segmented intake.
